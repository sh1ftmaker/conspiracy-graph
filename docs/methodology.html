How the scores are computed

Every theory in the Atlas carries three numbers — Truth, Impact and Notoriety — and none of them is a gut feeling. Each is produced by an explicit formula from factors that are annotated per theory and stored in the open repository, so any score can be traced back to its inputs. This page explains every calculation. The same breakdown appears inside each theory's detail panel under "⚙ Score breakdown."

Two different questions, two axes

A claim like "the CIA dosed unwitting citizens" is a question of historical fact — it either happened or it didn't, and evidence can settle it. A claim like "the universe is fundamentally conscious" is a framework of reality — no document could ever settle it. Scoring the second one with the machinery of the first is a category error, so the Atlas uses two axes:

  • Truth (green→red) — for empirical claims. "Did this actually happen?" Answered by a Bayesian combination of a plausibility prior and weighted evidence.
  • Plausibility (violet) — for metaphysical frameworks. "How credible is this as a model of reality, versus the alternatives?" It is capped well short of certainty, because nothing metaphysical is ever proven.

A theory's detail panel shows which axis is in use: a green "How true" bar and a violet "Plausibility" bar never appear on the same record.

Truth — Bayesian log-odds

Truth is computed as a logistic function of a sum in log-odds space. Working in log-odds means independent pieces of evidence simply add, and a single squashing function maps the total back to a 0–100 score:

Truth = 100 · σ( prior + Σ evidence − leak )   where σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e−x)

The prior sets where a claim starts before any evidence; the evidence terms push it up or down; the leak penalty accounts for the difficulty of keeping a large secret. Each is described below.

The prior — Means, Motive, Opportunity

The prior does not ask "how many theories in this genre turned out true?" The number of false theories in any genre is unlimited — imagination is free — so counting them would punish a real claim for the fanciful company it keeps. Instead the prior scores the specific claim on the classic investigator's triad: did the alleged actors have the means, the motive, and the opportunity? Each is annotated 0–4.

prior = −3.7 + (−0.6 − −3.7) · ( means · motive · opportunity )1/3 / 4

The three legs are combined as a geometric mean (a conjunction): a genuine zero on any one leg collapses the prior, because a conspiracy needs all three at once. Crucially, the result is capped below even odds — even a perfect 4/4/4 yields a prior of about −0.6 (≈ 35%), never 50%.

Why the ceiling is below 50%. Means, motive and opportunity establish that a conspiracy was possible — not that it happened. Plenty of actors had every capability and reason for crimes they never committed; opportunity is not occurrence. So even a maximally-plausible claim starts more likely false than true, and the evidence is what earns a high score. A physically impossible mechanism (flat earth, shapeshifting reptilians) pins the prior at the floor, −4.0, regardless of MMO.

Example contrast: "a global cabal secretly runs every nation" has a strong motive but near-zero means and opportunity (you cannot silently coordinate that many people), so its prior is crushed — not because other globalist theories are fringe, but on its own structural merits. A single firm rigging its own market data scores 4/4/4 and starts at the ceiling, where its evidence decides.

Evidence — credibility × stance

Each source contributes a weight equal to its credibility times its stance toward the claim. Not all sources are interchangeable kinds of proof, so every source is assigned a credibility class by its type and domain:

Source classWeight (of 5)

Stance is how the source bears on the claimed coordination — not merely on the surrounding event. (A news story titled "what happened in the outage" documents the outage; it says nothing about whether the outage was sabotage.) The strict stances and their log-odds strengths:

StanceStrengthMeaning

A source's weight is (class weight / 5) × stance strength. Several refinements keep any single source from dominating:

  • Diminishing returns. Within each side (for / against), sources are ranked and the k-th strongest is scaled by 1/k — the second corroborating source counts half, the third a third.
  • Independence. Multiple sources on the same domain count once (the strongest), so a story syndicated ten places is not ten pieces of evidence.
  • Cap. Each side's total is capped at ±6 in log-odds.
  • Proof floor. A source that flatly establishes the claim as fact ("proves") from a reasonably reliable venue is credited with the weight of the primary record it reports, even if the venue itself is only tertiary (e.g. an encyclopedia stating a declassified program existed).
  • Unsourced fallback. A record with no catalogued sources falls back to its listed evidence bullets at a small fixed weight, so it is not treated as having zero information.

Self-exculpatory sources — the defendant's denial

A source that is simply the accused party denying the accusation — "we didn't do it" — carries almost no probative weight, because a guilty party denies in exactly the same words as an innocent one. When a source is authored by (or officially speaks for) the very party a theory accuses, and its content is a denial or self-exoneration of that same charge, its evidence strength is discounted to 25% of normal.

Admissions against interest are the opposite. When the accused party admits fault, that is among the most credible evidence there is, and it keeps full weight. The discount applies only to self-serving denials, never to self-incriminating admissions — so a government declassifying its own misdeeds counts fully, while the same government's press release denying wrongdoing barely counts. Independent debunkings (journalists, academics, fact-checkers, courts, an unrelated agency) are not discounted; the rule targets "trust us, we didn't do it," not "here is independent proof it is false."

The leak penalty — Grimes' conspiracy-viability model

Big secrets are hard to keep. Following David Grimes' 2016 model (PLOS ONE), the expected number of leaks grows with the number of knowing conspirators and the years of required silence, so a claim requiring thousands of people to stay quiet for decades is penalized for having not yet leaked:

leak = p · 10(conspirator magnitude) · (years unbroken)   capped, and waived once a "proves" source exists

This bakes in a recency allowance: a claim about a recent event loses little for lacking leaked documents (there has been no time), while "ten thousand people faked the Moon landing for decades" is heavily penalized. Once a claim is actually proven, the penalty is irrelevant and dropped.

Plausibility — the metaphysical axis

Frameworks of reality (idealism, the simulation hypothesis, panpsychism, pantheism, and invented cosmologies) get their own formula. There is no "evidence" that a worldview happened; there is only how credible it is as a model, relative to competitors. Three factors, each 0–4:

  • Pedigree — who has taken it seriously, from internet invention up to a live position in academic philosophy or theoretical physics.
  • Coherence — internal consistency and compatibility with known physics and logic.
  • Parsimony — Occam's razor: how heavy an ontology it demands.
Plausibility = 100 · σ( pedigree + coherence + parsimony + argued support )   capped at 85

Here "evidence" means sources that argue for the framework — Bostrom on the simulation argument, Chalmers on panpsychism, the primary texts of a tradition — rather than proof it occurred. The score is capped at 85 because a framework of reality can never be proven, only made more or less compelling. These records render on the violet scale so they are never mistaken for established fact.

Impact — how much would it matter

Magnitude measures the consequence if the claim were true: how many people are affected (scale), how badly (severity), and how much of the institutional order is implicated (reach) — each 0–5, combined as a geometric mean so a claim must matter on all three dimensions to rank high, then gently recurved onto a 28–100 scale:

magnitude = recurve ( scale · severity · reach )1/3

But magnitude alone rewards scope over substance — a baseless extinction fantasy maxes every factor, while a proven cover-up scores only mid. So magnitude is tempered by plausibility: the theory’s own Truth (or, for frameworks, Plausibility) score pulls it down, so a claim can’t top the Impact axis on imagined stakes it hasn’t earned. The temper is a bounded nudge — it never fully crushes a genuine high-stakes claim, nor lifts one — so proven world-changers rise to the top and apocalyptic speculation settles into the middle rather than dominating it:

Impact = magnitude · [ 1 − 0.60 · ( 1 − (Truth/100)0.34 ) ]

Notoriety — measured, not guessed

Notoriety is how widely known a theory is — its cultural footprint, not its merit. It draws on two real signals, in order of preference:

1. Wikipedia pageviews, where the theory has an article: the trailing-12-month pageviews on a logarithmic scale (attention is power-law distributed).

Notoriety = 100 · ( log₁₀(annual pageviews) − 1.5 ) / 5.5

This is then softly blended toward the theory's attention tier (weight 0.8 on the measured value) with a lower floor but no upper cap — so genuinely high traffic is trusted and scores spread continuously instead of piling at a band edge, while a famous theory whose article link is a broken redirect can't be tanked to zero.

2. Researched web footprint, for the great majority of theories that will never have a Wikipedia article. Each was assessed from its real presence across the open web — Reddit threads, YouTube view counts, news coverage, search-result volume, TikTok — and placed on a calibrated 3–92 reach scale anchored to shared reference points (a household name like flat earth sits near the top; a deep-iceberg tile only a handful of forum posts ever mention sits near the bottom). This replaced a coarse six-rung tier ladder, so obscure theories are now individually ranked rather than snapped to one of six values.

Only if neither signal exists does a theory fall back to its bare attention tier.

The principles behind it

  • Fame is not evidence. A famous theory and an obscure one are scored by the same formula; notoriety feeds only the Notoriety axis, never Truth. Well-known but unproven claims score by their evidence, not their reach.
  • Plausibility is not truth. The prior can make a claim plausible; only evidence makes it true. This is why even perfect means/motive/opportunity starts below even odds.
  • Survivorship bias runs the other way too. Because proven conspiracies (MKUltra, COINTELPRO, Gulf of Tonkin) show these things do happen, a structurally similar unproven claim is not dismissed out of hand — it gets a fair prior and waits on evidence, rather than a genre penalty.
  • Denials from the accused are cheap. The party charged with the wrongdoing gets its say, but at a heavy discount; an admission against its own interest is what counts.
  • Everything is reproducible. The formulas and every annotated factor live in the public repository; scores are recomputed from them, not hand-set. If you disagree with a score, you can see exactly which input to argue with.

The Atlas catalogues theories to examine them, including ones it scores at zero; a high Impact or a catalogue entry is never an endorsement.
